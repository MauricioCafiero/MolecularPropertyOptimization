## Recent literature on fragment-based drug design (FBDD) (≈2020–2026)

Fragment-based drug discovery/design has continued to mature, with notable recent directions: (i) scaling of virtual fragment screening into ultra-large chemical spaces with ML triage; (ii) continued dominance and automation of high-throughput X-ray crystallographic screening (with improved weak-density detection); (iii) expansion of fragment workflows into cryo-EM for previously intractable targets; and (iv) rapid growth of covalent-fragment and chemoproteomics-enabled “ligandability” mapping directly in complex proteomes/cells. These trends are consistently emphasized across recent reviews and perspectives. ([1.1], [1.2], [2.1], [3.1])

### 1) Screening/validation modalities: increasingly integrated, orthogonal workflows
Recent reviews stress that fragment discovery is now typically performed with multiple orthogonal techniques, because fragment affinities are weak (often mM) and each method has different strengths in sensitivity, throughput, and ability to define binding poses and mechanisms. X-ray crystallography remains the “gold standard” for binding-mode definition, while NMR, SPR, ITC, HDX-MS, and native MS provide complementary information on binding, kinetics/thermodynamics, and/or mechanism. ([1.1], [1.2])

**Visual evidence:** the AlKharboush 2025 graphical review includes a workflow figure for how FBDD sits within the drug discovery pipeline and a table comparing common screening/biophysical methods used for fragments (advantages/limitations and typical use). ([1.3], [1.4], [1.5])

### 2) Structural methods: X-ray remains central; cryo-EM is becoming practical for fragments, but with clear constraints
High-throughput crystallographic fragment screening at dedicated beamlines/platforms, together with algorithms such as PanDDA for detecting low-occupancy/weak-density ligands, is highlighted as an enabling advance for large-scale structural fragment campaigns and rapid-response projects (e.g., COVID-era work). ([1.2])

Cryo-EM is increasingly positioned as a fragment-capable structural method for targets that are difficult to crystallize (large, dynamic, membrane proteins), but reviews emphasize that it is not yet routine for *primary* fragment screening. The key technical limitation is that, without a crystal lattice, conformational heterogeneity and low occupancy can cause fragment density to be averaged out unless fragments bind with high occupancy and a consistent pose across particles; image triage/clustering can add complexity. ([1.6])

Concrete cryo-EM fragment examples discussed in a 2024 cryo-EM drug-design review include β-galactosidase structures at ~2.2–2.3 Å with fragment-sized ligands visible in density, and a PKM2 fragment campaign incubating 68 ligands, where ~3.2 Å was reported as adequate to assign binding modes for fragment-sized molecules and distinguish them from noise; use of compound cocktails is described as a throughput strategy. ([4.1], [4.2])

A 2025 FBDD graphical review also notes an emerging direction of combining molecular docking and machine learning with lower-resolution cryo-EM maps (~3.5–4.5 Å) to help place fragments when density is ambiguous. ([1.6])

### 3) Native mass spectrometry (nMS): growing into a high-sensitivity, orthogonal fragment screen
A 2024 review describes how improvements in nMS instrumentation (mass resolution, desolvation, throughput, accessible molecular weight range) are enabling more robust fragment screening. The review also highlights progress toward challenging target classes such as membrane proteins (ion channels, GPCRs) using detergents and membrane mimetics (amphipols, bicelles, nanodiscs), and points to emerging hybrid nMS–cryo-EM directions for direct screening plus structural analysis. ([5.1])

### 4) Covalent fragments and chemoproteomics: movement from purified targets to proteomes/cells
A major 2020–2026 trend is the rise of “electrophile-first” covalent ligand discovery and covalent fragment libraries, increasingly paired with chemoproteomics to evaluate target engagement and proteome-wide selectivity. Reviews explicitly frame MS-proteomic workflows as a successful route to covalent fragment screening across proteomes. ([1.7], [1.2])

A representative primary-method paper (Biggs et al., 2025) reports a high-throughput label-free DIA chemoproteomics platform for competitive profiling of cysteine-reactive fragments against native proteomes. The method routinely identifies ~23,000 cysteines per run and ~32,000 cysteine sites across experiments; screening 80 chloroacetamide fragments across two cell lines yielded 438 liganded cysteine sites from 413 proteins, with follow-up concentration–response analysis and live-cell validation. ([6.1], [6.2])

A complementary chemoproteomics-first example (Vuorinen et al., 2025) uses family-focused ABPP (with a DUB probe) to screen 227 cysteine-reactive chloroacetamide fragments in HEK293T lysate at 200 µM, then couples hits to high-throughput chemistry direct-to-biology optimization to deliver an enantioselective covalent fragment for the DUB OTUD7B validated by chemoproteomics and biochemical assays. ([7.1])

### 5) Computational/AI-assisted FBDD: two parallel thrusts (ultralarge screening and AI-native fragment representations)
Recent perspectives highlight the practical uptake of ultra-large enumerated libraries (millions to billions) and their use in fragment-to-lead (F2L) projects, often via docking/virtual screening and hit expansion strategies. In a 2024 annual F2L perspective summarizing 2022 case studies, virtual screening/docking appeared in ~50% of reported examples, and ultra-large screens (e.g., 3.5 million and 235 million virtual compounds, and 12,800-fragment sets) are discussed as a major operational shift in how fragment hits are grown and optimized. ([3.1], [3.2])

AI/ML are also increasingly used to reduce computational burden: a 2025 graphical FBDD review describes ML models trained on initial docking results to prioritize chemical space and reduce explicit docking work by up to ~100-fold in some workflows. ([1.1], [1.8])

On the “AI-native” side, new work is focusing on how to define fragments for machine learning and generative design. DigFrag 2024 is an attention-based, data-driven fragmentation method that produces fragments with higher diversity than common rule-based methods and can improve outputs of deep generative models; reported overlap with other fragmentation methods was low (≈10–21% for drugs), and mean fragment MW was ~137. ([8.1])

A 2024 Communications Chemistry review further frames fragmentation as a foundational step for AI-enabled drug discovery, compiling fragmentation algorithms and linking fragmentation choices to downstream tasks such as molecular generation and property prediction. ([9.1])
