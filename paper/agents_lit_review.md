# Large Language Models for Molecular Design: A Literature Review

## Overview

The application of general-purpose large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4, ChatGPT, Llama, and PaLM to molecular design has emerged as a rapidly growing field since 2023. Unlike domain-specific transformer models trained exclusively on SMILES strings, these approaches leverage the broad knowledge, reasoning, and in-context learning capabilities of general-purpose LLMs for tasks spanning de novo molecule generation, molecular optimization, property prediction, and autonomous experimental design. The literature reveals several distinct paradigms for deploying LLMs in this context, which can be organized into five major categories: 1 tool-augmented chemistry agents, 2 direct molecular generation via prompting and retrieval, 3 fine-tuned general-purpose LLMs, 4 multi-agent systems, and 5 LLMs as evolutionary or optimization operators.

## 1. Tool-Augmented Chemistry Agents

The most prominent approach involves augmenting general-purpose LLMs with external chemistry tools, enabling them to overcome their inherent limitations in chemical reasoning and computation. **ChemCrow** is a pioneering example: it couples GPT-4 with 18 expert-designed tools organized into general utilities (web search, literature search, Python execution), molecular tools (name-to-SMILES conversion, property calculation, purchasability checks, patent verification, molecule modification via medicinal chemistry reactions), and chemical reaction tools ([1.1], [1.2]). In expert evaluation, ChemCrow outperformed standalone GPT-4 (9.24/10 vs 4.79/10) on chemical factuality, reasoning, and completeness, and autonomously planned and supported the synthesis of an insect repellent, three organocatalysts, and a novel chromophore ([2.1]).

**Coscientist**, published in *Nature*, represents perhaps the most ambitious demonstration. This GPT-4-driven system autonomously designs, plans, and executes complex experiments using modular commands for web/documentation search, Python code execution, and laboratory hardware control ([3.1]). It was evaluated on six diverse tasks, with the most complex being the successful optimization of palladium-catalyzed cross-coupling reactions, demonstrating that LLMs can interface directly with automated laboratory equipment ([2.2]).

These systems reflect a broader trend where LLMs serve as orchestrators that combine reasoning with specialized computational and experimental tools, compensating for the LLM's limitations in precise chemical computation while leveraging its planning and language understanding capabilities ([4.1], [4.2]).

## 2. Direct Molecular Generation via Prompting and Retrieval

Several studies have explored using general-purpose LLMs directly for molecular generation through prompting strategies. **MolReGPT** introduced an "In-Context Few-Shot Molecule Learning" paradigm where similar molecules and their textual descriptions are retrieved from a database and provided as context examples to ChatGPT, enabling molecule-caption translation and text-based molecule generation without any domain-specific fine-tuning. MolReGPT outperformed MolT5-base and was comparable to MolT5-large ([5.1], [6.1]).

**LMLF (Language Models with Logical Feedback)** takes a different approach by separating prompts into machine-checkable logical constraints (encoding drug-design rules such as molecular weight, logP, synthetic accessibility, and binding energy thresholds) and a simple text query. Molecules generated by GPT-3 and PaLM are iteratively verified against these constraints, with the logical specifications refined through feedback. On JAK2 and DRD2 targets, LMLF-generated molecules showed distributions skewed toward higher predicted binding affinities compared to both baselines and runs without logical feedback, with a computational chemist assessing some outputs as potentially novel inhibitors ([7.1], [7.2]).

However, benchmarking studies have revealed important limitations of direct prompting. Guo et al. found that while GPT-4 generated mostly chemically valid molecules (>89%), exact-match accuracy to ground-truth molecules was below 20%, and performance on reaction prediction and retrosynthesis was substantially lower than specialized models ([8.1], [8.2]). Zhang et al. reported that GPT-4 achieved a 48.39% success rate for QED-targeted molecular optimization but found a 0% success rate for generating molecules matching detailed textual descriptions, with generated SMILES failing to match provided structural specifications ([9.1], [9.2]). General-purpose decoder LLMs showed only ~27% accuracy on name-to-SMILES conversion and ~31% mean relative error on partition coefficient prediction ([2.3]).

## 3. Fine-Tuned General-Purpose LLMs

A productive middle ground involves fine-tuning general-purpose LLMs on molecular data while retaining their broad capabilities. **SmileyLlama** converts Meta's Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct into a chemical language model through supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on approximately 2 million ChEMBL molecules with RDKit-derived property annotations, followed by Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) using paired preferred/non-preferred molecular outputs (~76,000 training pairs). The DPO step substantially improved satisfaction of property constraints for directed chemical space exploration, including drug-relevant properties like H-bond donors/acceptors, molecular weight, and ClogP ([10.1], [10.2]).

**ChatMol** addresses a critical limitation of LLMs—poor numerical recognition—by incorporating position-encoding-inspired numerical encodings within prompts. Combined with task-specific prompting and feedback learning from property predictors, ChatMol consistently outperformed VAE and RL-based baselines across single-property, substructure-property, and multi-property constrained tasks, achieving a KD value of 0.25 for the protein target ESR1 in multi-objective binding affinity maximization and improving Pearson correlation between instructed and generated property values by up to 0.49 ([11.1], [11.2]).

These approaches demonstrate that general-purpose LLMs can serve as strong foundation models for chemical language modeling when appropriately adapted, with the survey literature identifying supervised fine-tuning, in-context molecule tuning (ICMT), and property-enhanced instruction tuning (PEIT) as the major adaptation strategies ([12.1]).

## 4. Multi-Agent Systems

Multi-agent architectures represent an increasingly sophisticated paradigm. **PharmAgents** simulates a virtual pharmaceutical ecosystem where specialist LLM agents handle distinct roles—disease analysis, target analysis, pocket analysis, lead identification (via structure-based design, virtual screening, and LLM-guided generation), and iterative lead optimization through an interaction analysis → design → generation → reflection → selection loop using docking software, PLIP, and RDKit ([13.1]).

**MT-Mol** assembles 154 RDKit-based chemistry functions into five specialized analyst agents, with additional scientist, verifier, and reviewer agents that decompose molecular optimization into tool-grounded iterative steps. It achieved state-of-the-art performance on 15 of 23 tasks from the PMO-1K molecular optimization benchmark, outperforming prior LLM-based optimization systems ([14.1]).

An **Auditable Agent Platform** demonstrates the importance of transparency in these systems, using hierarchical agents (Principal Researcher, Database Agent, Medicinal Chemist, Ranking Agent, Scientific Critic) that record full provenance of molecular lineage and decision-making. The multi-agent architecture improved predicted binding affinity by 31% compared to unguided approaches, though it revealed a trade-off between potency-seeking and broader drug-like properties ([15.1], [15.2]).

## 5. LLMs as Optimization and Design Consultants

A distinctive use case involves LLMs serving as intellectual partners rather than direct molecular generators. Wang et al. demonstrated this in a case study on anticocaine addiction drug development, where GPT-4 was deployed in three persona-based roles—idea generation, mathematical/statistical methodology clarification, and coding assistance. GPT-4 proposed the integration of stochastic Langevin-equation-based optimization into a Generative Network Complex, recommended multi-target optimization strategies, suggested similarity metrics for latent-space molecular comparison, and assisted with code development and debugging ([16.1], [16.2], [16.3]). The resulting pipeline generated 330 candidate molecules targeting DAT, NET, and SERT receptors, which were narrowed to 15 multi-target leads through affinity prediction, docking, Lipinski filtering, and ADMET screening ([16.4], [16.5], [16.6]).

Additionally, LLMs have been used as evolutionary operators within population-based optimization frameworks (MOLLEO), where the LLM performs mutation and crossover operations on molecular populations ([12.2]). LLMs have also been shown to synthesize interpretable rules from data and literature (LLM4SD) that can improve the performance of conventional machine learning models for molecular property prediction tasks ([17.1]).

## Key Limitations and Challenges

Several cross-cutting limitations emerge from the literature:

1. **Limited SMILES comprehension**: General-purpose LLMs struggle with the precise syntax and semantics of molecular string representations. Even state-of-the-art online LLMs have difficulty replicating incomplete molecules, and existing molecular representations are considered suboptimal for LLM processing ([11.2], [2.3]).

2. **Numerical handling deficits**: LLMs exhibit poor numerical recognition and reasoning, necessitating specialized encodings and prompt engineering to achieve property-conditioned generation ([11.1]).

3. **Hallucination and reliability**: LLM outputs can be unreliable for precise chemical tasks, requiring validation through external tools, computational chemistry verification, and expert review ([6.1], [18.1]).

4. **Reproducibility concerns**: Dependence on closed-source, frequently updated commercial LLM APIs creates challenges for scientific reproducibility ([2.3]).

5. **Performance gap with specialized models**: On most quantitative chemistry benchmarks, general-purpose LLMs still lag behind domain-specific models—for example, GPT-4 achieved 80% on Buchwald-Hartwig yield prediction compared to 96% for specialized UAGNN ([8.1], [8.2]).

## Conclusion

The use of general-purpose LLMs for molecular design is a rapidly evolving field that has moved beyond simple proof-of-concept demonstrations to increasingly sophisticated multi-agent, tool-augmented, and fine-tuned systems. The most successful approaches do not rely on LLMs alone but integrate them with computational chemistry tools, property predictors, docking software, and experimental automation. While standalone LLMs remain limited in their ability to reliably generate valid, targeted molecules, they excel as reasoning engines, workflow orchestrators, and knowledge integrators that can substantially accelerate drug discovery pipelines when properly augmented with domain-specific capabilities ([18.1], [4.2]).
